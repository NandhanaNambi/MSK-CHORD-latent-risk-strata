Where Is the Survival Risk Hiding?

This project explores survival heterogeneity in 15,871 patients from the MSK-CHORD cohort by combining latent clinicogenomic risk stratification with adjusted genomic survival modeling.

Read report Code Data

About

The MSK-CHORD cohort is a large clinicogenomic cancer dataset containing integrated clinical variables, genomic mutation features, and survival outcomes. This project explores survival heterogeneity by combining latent patient risk stratification with adjusted genomic survival modeling.

A patient-level analytic dataset was constructed using age, tumor stage, cancer type, overall survival time, survival event status, and binary mutation indicators for selected cancer driver genes. These features were used to identify hidden clinicogenomic patient subgroups and evaluate whether the resulting clusters corresponded to distinct survival risk profiles.

The analysis also evaluates selected driver-gene mutations, representative pathway-level alteration groups, and cumulative driver mutation burden in relation to overall survival. Together, these approaches connect unsupervised subgroup discovery with clinically adjusted survival modeling.

Methods

A clinicogenomic feature matrix was constructed using age, tumor stage, and binary mutation indicators for selected cancer driver genes. Continuous variables were standardized prior to analysis to ensure comparability across features. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was applied to reduce dimensionality and visualize structure in the feature space.

K-means clustering was used to identify latent patient subgroups, with the number of clusters selected using the elbow method based on within-cluster variation. Following cluster assignment, overall survival differences were evaluated using Kaplan-Meier estimation and compared using the log-rank test. Cox proportional hazards regression was used to quantify the association between cluster membership and survival risk.

Additional Cox proportional hazards models were fit to evaluate selected driver-gene mutations, representative pathway-level alteration groups, and cumulative driver mutation burden in relation to overall survival. These models were adjusted for age and tumor stage and stratified by cancer type to account for broad differences across cancer diagnoses. Hazard ratios were interpreted as relative mortality hazard estimates associated with each genomic feature or mutation-burden category.

  • Primary outcome: overall survival
  • Feature matrix: age, tumor stage, cancer type, and selected driver-gene mutation indicators
  • Dimensionality reduction: principal component analysis
  • Clustering method: K-means clustering
  • Cluster selection: elbow method
  • Survival methods: Kaplan-Meier estimation, log-rank test, and Cox proportional hazards regression
  • Genomic modeling: selected driver genes, pathway-level alteration groups, and mutation burden
  • Software: R

Integrated Interpretation

Together, the latent risk stratification and genomic survival modeling analyses provide complementary views of survival heterogeneity in the MSK-CHORD cohort. The clustering analysis identified broad clinicogenomic patient subgroups, while the Cox models evaluated whether selected mutations, pathway-level alteration groups, and mutation burden were associated with overall survival after clinical adjustment.

Limitations

This analysis is based on selected driver genes rather than a complete genome-wide mutation profile. The pathway-level groups are representative summaries and should not be interpreted as full pathway annotations. Because the cohort is observational, associations should not be interpreted as causal. Cluster assignments also depend on the selected input features, preprocessing choices, and clustering method.

Key Findings

  • The final analytic cohort included 15,871 patients across five cancer types, with 5,689 observed death events and an overall event rate of 35.8%.
  • Latent risk stratification identified three clinicogenomic patient subgroups with distinct survival patterns, clinical characteristics, and mutation profiles.
  • In adjusted gene-level Cox models, TP53 and KRAS mutations were associated with higher mortality hazard, while APC and EGFR mutations were associated with lower estimated hazard in this cohort.
  • Pathway-level modeling suggested higher hazard for selected p53/genomic instability and MAPK-related alteration groups, while the selected Wnt-related alteration group showed lower estimated hazard.
  • Patients with one or more selected driver-gene mutations had higher adjusted mortality hazard compared with patients with zero selected driver-gene mutations.
  • Overall, the project shows how unsupervised subgroup discovery and adjusted survival modeling can provide complementary views of survival heterogeneity in a clinicogenomic cancer cohort.
